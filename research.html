Research Overview

My research explores the complex interplay between economic behavior, self and social image, and decision-making, particularly focusing on how self-deception, altruism, and expectations influence individual choices. Through experimental and theoretical approaches, I examine how these factors drive decisions in various contexts, from organizations to public policy preferences, and how they can lead to suboptimal outcomes.

Key Research Areas

I investigate three main areas: (1) the role of self-deception and altruism in shaping prosocial behavior, examining when individuals act out of genuine concern versus image-driven motives and the potential side-effects; (2) the impact of social image concerns on task selection, exploring how individualsâ€™ desire for reputation influences their willingness to undertake challenges; and (3) how expectations shape attitudes towards redistribution, revealing how beliefs interact with economic policy preferences.
